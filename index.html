Five Quality Secondary Sources

1

Pearl Harbor: From Infamy to Greatness

Craig Nelson

Nelson, C. (2016). Pearl Harbor: From infamy to greatness. Scribner.

Comprehensive account of the attack and U.S. response, examining both military and civilian perspectives.

2

Day of Infamy

Walter Lord

Lord, W. (1957). Day of infamy. Henry Holt & Co.

Classic narrative of the events and aftermath, considered one of the definitive accounts of December 7, 1941.

3

At Dawn We Slept: The Untold Story of Pearl Harbor

Gordon W. Prange

Prange, G. W. (1981). At dawn we slept: The untold story of Pearl Harbor. McGraw-Hill.

In-depth analysis of causes, execution, and consequences based on extensive research and interviews.

4

Pearl Harbor: The Verdict of History

Gordon W. Prange, Donald M. Goldstein, Katherine V. Dillon

Prange, G. W., Goldstein, D. M., & Dillon, K. V. (1986). Pearl Harbor: The verdict of history. McGraw-Hill.

Explores responsibility and impact on U.S. policy, examining intelligence failures and decision-making.

5

December 1941: 31 Days that Changed America and Saved the World

Craig Shirley

Shirley, C. (2011). December 1941: 31 days that changed America and saved the world. Thomas Nelson.

Examines U.S. reaction and policy changes post-attack, covering the entire month of December 1941.

Primary Sources

Original documents, photographs, and records from December 7, 1941, and its aftermath.

Government Document - 1946

Report of the Joint Committee on the Investigation of the Pearl Harbor Attack

United States Congress. (1946). Report of the Joint Committee on the Investigation of the Pearl Harbor Attack. U.S. Government Printing Office.

Official government investigation into the causes and failures surrounding the attack. This comprehensive report examined intelligence breakdowns, military preparedness, and decision-making processes that led to the surprise attack.

Presidential Address - December 8, 1941

FDR's "Day of Infamy" Speech

Roosevelt, F. D. (1941, December 8). Address to Congress Requesting a Declaration of War with Japan. U.S. Government.

President Roosevelt's historic speech to Congress, delivered one day after the attack. This address marked the United States' entry into World War II and remains one of the most significant presidential speeches in American history.

Government Statement - December 7, 1941

Japanese Announcement of the Attack at Pearl Harbor

Hirohito. (1941). Japanese announcement of the attack at Pearl Harbor. Gilder Lehrman Institute of American History.

Official Japanese government statement on the attack, providing insight into the Japanese perspective and justification for military action against the United States.

Naval Communication - December 7, 1941

"This is Not Drill" Naval Dispatch

Commander in Chief, U.S. Pacific Fleet. (1941, December 7). "This is Not Drill" Naval Dispatch. U.S. Navy.

First official U.S. military communication announcing the attack. This urgent dispatch alerted all forces that Pearl Harbor was under genuine attack, not a training exercise.

Official U.S. Navy Photograph - December 7, 1941

USS Arizona Burning

U.S. Navy. (1941, December 7). USS Arizona burning after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor. U.S. Navy.

Iconic photograph documenting the destruction during the attack. The USS Arizona remains submerged at Pearl Harbor, serving as a memorial to the 1,177 sailors and Marines who lost their lives aboard the ship.

Historical Analysis: A Turning Point in American History

The Argument

The attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, was not merely a military strike but a pivotal moment that transformed American foreign policy, ended U.S. isolationism, and united a divided nation in a common cause. The event's significance lies not only in its immediate military impact but in how it fundamentally reshaped America's role on the world stage.

Key Details

The Attack

  • 353 Japanese aircraft launched in two waves
  • 8 battleships damaged or destroyed
  • 2,403 Americans killed, 1,178 wounded
  • Attack lasted approximately 90 minutes
  • Occurred at 7:48 AM Hawaiian time

Intelligence Failures

  • Intercepted Japanese communications not properly analyzed
  • Radar detection dismissed as training error
  • Lack of coordination between Army and Navy
  • Diplomatic negotiations ongoing when attack occurred
  • Underestimation of Japanese military capability

Immediate Consequences

  • U.S. declaration of war on December 8, 1941
  • Germany and Italy declared war on U.S. December 11
  • Mobilization of 16 million American troops
  • Shift to total war economy and production
  • Japanese-American internment (Executive Order 9066)

Timeline of Events

1940-1941

Rising Tensions

U.S. imposes oil embargo on Japan; diplomatic negotiations deteriorate as Japan expands in Asia.

November 26, 1941

Japanese Fleet Departs

Japanese carrier strike force leaves Hitokappu Bay under radio silence, heading toward Hawaii.

December 7, 1941 - 7:48 AM

Attack Begins

First wave of Japanese aircraft strikes Pearl Harbor; "This is not drill" message sent.

December 7, 1941 - 9:00 AM

Attack Ends

Second wave completes assault; Japanese forces withdraw with minimal losses.

December 8, 1941

"Day of Infamy" Speech

President Roosevelt addresses Congress; U.S. declares war on Japan with only one dissenting vote.

December 11, 1941

Global War

Germany and Italy declare war on United States, bringing America fully into World War II.

1942-1945

American Mobilization

U.S. transforms into "Arsenal of Democracy," producing unprecedented military and industrial output.

Causes and Effects

Contributing Causes

  • Japanese Expansionism: Japan's invasion of China and Southeast Asia threatened American interests in the Pacific.
  • Economic Sanctions: U.S. oil embargo crippled Japan's military and industrial capacity, forcing a decision for war.
  • Failed Diplomacy: Breakdown of negotiations between Japanese diplomats and U.S. Secretary of State Cordell Hull.
  • Strategic Calculation: Japan believed a decisive strike would demoralize America and allow Japanese dominance in Asia.
  • American Unpreparedness: Despite war warnings, Pearl Harbor was not on full alert and defenses were inadequate.

Long-term Effects

  • End of Isolationism: America permanently abandoned its isolationist stance and became a global superpower.
  • United Nations: U.S. leadership in creating international institutions for collective security.
  • Nuclear Age: The war led to development of atomic weapons and fundamentally changed warfare.
  • Cold War: U.S.-Soviet alliance during WWII transformed into decades-long ideological conflict.
  • American Century: U.S. emerged as dominant economic and military power, shaping global order.

Historical Significance

The attack on Pearl Harbor represents a watershed moment in American and world history. It transformed the United States from a nation reluctant to engage in "foreign entanglements" into the leader of the free world. The attack unified American public opinion overnight‚Äîpolling showed support for war jumped from 15% to over 90% within days.

The military failures and intelligence breakdowns exposed at Pearl Harbor led to fundamental reforms in U.S. military structure, including better inter-service cooperation and the eventual creation of the Department of Defense and Central Intelligence Agency. The congressional investigation established important precedents for governmental accountability and oversight.

Perhaps most significantly, Pearl Harbor demonstrated that geographic isolation could no longer protect America from global conflicts. This realization shaped U.S. foreign policy for generations, leading to permanent military alliances, forward deployment of forces, and active engagement in international affairs‚Äîpolicies that continue to define American strategy today.
